Section 02

Introduction - The AV Insurance Problem

The automobile insurance industry was built on a simple premise: human drivers make mistakes, and insurers price that risk. Over 90% of all traditional vehicle accidents in the United States are attributable to human error - distraction, impairment, fatigue, and misjudgement. The rise of AI-driven autonomous vehicles fundamentally disrupts this model.

Autonomous vehicles operate across a six-level taxonomy defined by SAE International (J3016) and adopted by the NHTSA. This report focuses on Level 2 through Level 5 systems that are currently deployed on US public roads:

SAE Level Classification Human Role Examples (USA 2026) Insurance Type
L2 Partial Automation Always supervising Tesla Autopilot, GM Super Cruise, Volvo Pilot Assist Personal Auto
L2+ Enhanced Partial Always supervising Tesla FSD (Supervised), Mercedes Drive Pilot (limited) Personal + Hybrid
L3 Conditional Automation Ready to intervene Honda Traffic Jam Pilot (Japan only), limited US pilots Hybrid
L4 High Automation Not required (geofenced) Waymo One (SF, Phoenix, LA), Cruise (suspended 2023) Product Liability
L5 Full Automation No driver needed Not yet commercially available in USA Future Product

The central insurance challenge is the liability shift: as control migrates from human operator to machine algorithm, legal and financial responsibility moves from personal auto policies toward product liability claims against manufacturers and software developers. This creates a multi-party coverage environment that traditional actuarial models were not designed to handle.

Why the USA Is the Critical Market

The United States leads the world in AV deployment for three reasons: (1) permissive state-level testing laws in California, Arizona, Texas and Florida; (2) the concentration of technology companies (Waymo, Tesla, Cruise, Aurora, Zoox) in Silicon Valley and the Sun Belt; and (3) the NHTSA's June 2021 Standing General Order on crash reporting, which created the world's most comprehensive public AV incident database. As of 2026, California alone has recorded over 761 ADS crashes - more than 60% of the national total - making it the primary actuarial laboratory for this product.

Section 07 - Statistical Models

GLM - Generalised Linear Model (Crash Frequency)

A Generalised Linear Model with a Negative Binomial distribution is the industry-standard approach for modelling insurance claim frequency when overdispersion is present (variance > mean), which is the case in AV crash data due to clustering effects in dense urban ODD zones and across Tesla's uniquely large reporting footprint.

Model Specification

Y_i ~ NegBin(μ_i, α) log(μ_i) = β₀ + β₁·Year + β₂·FleetSize_i + β₃·AVLevel_i + β₄·State_i + β₅·Seasonality_i + offset(log(VMT_i))
CovariateCoefficient (β)SEDirectionInterpretation
Intercept (β₀) −4.82 0.31 - Baseline log-rate at 2021 reference level
Year Trend (β₁) +0.24 0.04 ↑ Absolute crash count increases ~27% per year (fleet-driven)
Fleet Size (β₂) +0.61 0.07 ↑ Dominant driver: every 10K vehicles adds ~6.1% to expected crashes
AV Level L4 vs L2 (β₃) −1.43 0.18 ↓ L4 systems have ~76% lower frequency rate per VMT vs L2
California Indicator (β₄) +0.89 0.12 ↑ ~143% higher reporting/exposure rate vs baseline state
Q4 Seasonality (β₅) +0.15 0.09 ↑ Modest Q4 elevation (rain, holiday traffic, holiday fleet expansion)
Dispersion (α) 2.14 0.22 - Confirms overdispersion; Negative Binomial appropriate over Poisson
Actuarial Insight: The negative sign on the L4 coefficient (−1.43) is the single most important finding for starscream Saver's pricing strategy. It indicates that fully autonomous Level 4 vehicles, when VMT-normalised, represent substantially lower claim frequency risk than L2 ADAS - but the individual claim severity is higher when incidents do occur (due to product liability loading and technology repair costs).

Section 08

GAM - Generalised Additive Model (Non-linear Trend)

The GLM's linear trend assumption is potentially too restrictive for AV crash data, which exhibits technology maturity effects, regulatory disruptions (e.g., the Cruise suspension), and software update cycles. A Generalised Additive Model (GAM) using thin-plate regression splines allows the relationship between time and crash rate to be non-linear.

Model Specification

log(E[Y_i]) = α + f₁(Year) + f₂(VMT) + f₃(FleetAVLevel) + ε_i where f₁, f₂, f₃ are smooth spline functions estimated from data

GAM Smooth: Crash Rate Trend (Non-linear fit, 2014–2025)

The smooth reveals three phases: (1) 2014–2018 rapid rise with ADAS adoption; (2) 2018–2021 plateau during L4 testing scale-up; (3) 2021–present expansion with mandatory reporting.

Model: GAM with Thin-Plate Splines | edf (effective degrees of freedom) ≈ 4.2 | Deviance explained = 78.3%

Smooth Term Interpretation

The spline for the Year smooth term (f₁) shows an estimated inflection point around 2022–2023, consistent with the peak of the Autopilot investigation and Cruise suspension. The GAM predicts a moderate deceleration in crash-rate growth from 2026 onward, driven by:

(a) Improved software updates post-2023 Autopilot recall; (b) Greater regulatory scrutiny reducing aggressive L2 deployment; (c) Increasing share of L4 systems (lower per-VMT rate) in the overall AV fleet mix.

However, the model retains significant uncertainty in the 2026–2030 window because the post-recall effectiveness of Tesla's OTA update remains unvalidated at scale, and Waymo's fleet is expanding rapidly into new cities.

Section 09

EVT - Extreme Value Theory (Tail Risk & Catastrophic Claims)

Standard GLM/GAM models capture expected loss well but underestimate tail risk - the probability of large, catastrophic claims arising from systemic software failures, recall events, or fleet-wide cyber incidents. Extreme Value Theory (EVT) using the Peaks Over Threshold (POT) method with a Generalised Pareto Distribution (GPD) is appropriate for this analysis.

Model Specification - GPD Tail Model

For excess losses above threshold u: P(X > u + y | X > u) ≈ (1 + ξy/σ)^(−1/ξ) [Generalised Pareto Distribution] Shape parameter ξ: determines tail heaviness Scale parameter σ: threshold exceedance scale Threshold u: selected at 90th percentile of claim distribution

Liability Shift - Financial Impact on Premiums

The Casualty Actuarial Society (2018) modelled a full product-liability regime for all vehicles and found that shifting from personal auto to product liability would approximately double or triple the average vehicle insurance premium (from ~$781 to $1,578–$2,355 in 2011 dollars). This is because product liability insurance carries far higher frictional costs: expert witnesses, forensic software analysis, longer litigation timelines, and greater aggregate exposure.

For a 2026 baseline of approximately $1,800–$2,200 average personal auto premium, the equivalent product-liability-loaded AV premium could reach $3,500–$5,500 per vehicle per year for L4 operators in the transitional period - before sufficient actuarial data exists to validate lower loss rates and drive premiums down.

starscream Saver Opportunity: By being an early mover with data-first underwriting (telematics, OEM API data, software version tracking), starscream Saver can build a proprietary actuarial edge before competitors have sufficient historical data to accurately price this risk class.

Insurance Premium Pricing Framework

starscream Saver requires a pricing framework that accounts for the novelty of AV risk, the multi-party liability environment, and the rapidly improving safety trajectory of high-automation systems. The following framework is recommended.

Ratemaking Formula

P = (ELF × LDF × IBNR) + PL_Loading + EVT_Loading + Cyber_Sub-limit + Expense where: ELF = Expected Loss Frequency (from GLM, per VMT exposure unit) LDF = Loss Development Factor (tail factors; AV litigation 3–7 yr) IBNR = Incurred But Not Reported (high for AV; 25–40% loading suggested) PL = Product Liability loading (function of AV level, ODD complexity) EVT = Extreme Value tail loading (GPD-derived; 8–15% of base premium) Cyber= Cyber/software risk sub-limit loading Expense = Acquisition + Administration + Profit margin

Policy Structure & Coverage Design

starscream Saver's AV insurance product should be structured as a modular, technology-aware policy that dynamically adjusts coverage attribution based on the vehicle's operating mode at the time of loss. The following layered structure is recommended.

Coverage Layers

LayerCoverage NameTriggerLimit (Indicative)Notes
Layer 1 Human Operator Liability Vehicle in manual mode at time of loss $250K–$1M CSL Standard personal auto form
Layer 2 AV System Liability (Tech Mode) ADAS/ADS engaged; system had control $1M–$5M per occurrence Product liability form; telematics-verified mode
Layer 3 Product & Manufacturer Liability Wrap Design/software defect; OEM recall event $5M–$25M aggregate Wrap policy; subrogation rights to OEM
Layer 4 Cyber & Software Risk Vehicle compromised by cyber/software failure $2M sub-limit Cyber exclusion buy-back; fleet-wide event cap
Optional Physical Damage - Sensor Coverage Damage to AV sensors (LiDAR, radar, cameras) Actual cash value + $8K sensor uplift ADAS/ADS sensor repair costs typically 2–4× standard

Telematics Data Requirements

For starscream Saver to accurately assign liability layer and calculate premium, the following telematics data should be mandatory for AV policyholders:

Required: Operating mode at time of event (manual / L2 ADAS / L4 ADS); software version active; GPS coordinates and timestamp; event data recorder (EDR) log; sensor health status; speed, acceleration, and braking data for 60 seconds pre-event; driver monitoring system output (attention detection where available).

Optional (for discount eligibility): Continuous telematics driving behaviour score; OTA update compliance flag; annual safety mode engagement percentage.

Section 16

Conclusions & Recommendations

Key Actuarial Findings

1. The absolute crash count is rising, but the rate is falling. As AV fleet size grows (S&P Global forecasts 87M L2+ vehicles by 2035), total reported incidents will increase. But per-VMT crash rates are declining, particularly for L4 systems (Waymo: −92% bodily injury claims vs human, Swiss Re 2024). Pricing must use exposure-based (per-mile) rather than flat-rate models.

2. Liability is shifting - but not uniformly or quickly. The GLM model confirms L4 systems carry ~76% lower frequency per VMT vs L2. However, individual claim severity is higher due to product liability litigation multipliers. The CAS estimates product liability can double or triple average premiums in a full AV world. starscream Saver must price this tail adequately.

3. EVT analysis reveals catastrophic risk is real and under-priced industry-wide. The heavy-tailed GPD fit (ξ = 0.42) indicates that systemic events - recall-scale software defects, fleet-wide cyber compromise - present materially unbounded loss scenarios. CAT reinsurance and aggregate stop-loss provisions are non-negotiable.

4. Data quality is the primary competitive moat. The company with the best telematics data - operating mode, software version, EDR logs - will out-price and out-select competitors. Mandatory telematics for all AV policyholders is both an underwriting necessity and a product differentiator.
